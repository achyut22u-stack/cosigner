6. Why A US Loan With A US Cosigner

  1. Lower interest rates. US private student loans for top schools price between 3% and 9% fixed; comparable Indian loans are 9% to 14%. On a $150K, 10-year loan, that's roughly USD 60,000 to 70,000 in lifetime interest savings.
  2. Access to HKS's Loan Repayment Assistance Program (LRAP). If I take a public-sector or nonprofit role after graduation, HKS covers 25% to 100% of my monthly loan payment (scaled to income, capped at $100K single / $125K married, for up to 5 years). This doesn't apply to consulting roles, but it's still a good scheme to be eligible for.
  3. Year 2 funding plan. The US loan only needs to cover Year 1. For Year 2, my plan in order of preference is fellowships first, then RA-ships and internships, and only if needed a parallel India loan in my mother's name (~INR 75 lakh, ~USD 90K).

Reference: HKS Loan Repayment Assistance Program (LRAP)

9. What Being A Cosigner Actually Means For You

  • Joint and several liability. If I miss a payment or default, the lender can pursue you for the full balance, but based on my backup plans, I do not expect this eventuality to happen!
  • Credit reporting. The loan and its payment history will appear on your credit report. While I'm paying on time, this is generally credit-positive or neutral. A late or missed payment by me would impact your credit score.
  • Borrowing capacity. Until the loan is refinanced into my name only, the outstanding balance counts toward your debt-to-income ratio when you apply for new credit (a mortgage refinance, for example).
  • Bankruptcy. US private student loans aren't generally dischargeable in bankruptcy, for either of us.
  • Cosigner release. Most US private lenders allow cosigner release after a defined number of on-time payments (typically 24 to 48 months) plus a creditworthiness check. My plan is faster: a full refinance in my name only within ~2 months of starting work, which fully removes you from the loan.
  • Death or disability discharge. Most lenders include a death and permanent disability discharge for the borrower, which would also release you. I'll confirm this clause is in whichever lender we ultimately go with.

10. Next Steps And The Application Process

I'm working with an education loan consultant in India who has direct relationships with US-based lenders that lend to Harvard graduate students. The process from here:

  1. Submit applications to two or three of MPower, Earnest, Discover, and HUCU in parallel.
  2. Compare offers on rate, fees, deferment, cosigner release, and prepayment terms; pick the best combination.
  3. The application step is non-binding. No application fee, no processing fee, no liability of any kind for either of us until we formally accept an offer.
  4. Even after acceptance, interest only accrues on what I actually disburse. I plan to draw in tranches, only as needed. Any unused balance (from fellowships, TA/RA-ships, etc.) I simply won't draw down.
  5. I'll share the final approval disclosure with you for review before we sign anything.
